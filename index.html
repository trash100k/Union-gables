I The Mansion

Old Saratoga,
kept exactly.

Built around 1901, when the racing crowd came north by private rail and the town wore its money quietly. A Queen Anne Victorian on Union Avenue — turrets, a wraparound porch, and rooms furnished in their period antiques. Nothing reproduced, nothing rushed.

People came to Saratoga long before the horses did. It was the Queen of the Spas, and the carbonated mineral springs drew the carriage trade to take the waters decades before a flat track existed — a heritage that still runs year-round at the Roosevelt Baths in the spa park. Then, in 1863, John Morrissey organized racing on this very avenue; the Travers followed in 1864, and it has run every season since — the oldest organized major sporting venue in the country. The grand houses along Union Avenue are what that era left behind, and this is one of them.

Beyond the parlor: better than an acre of perennial gardens, a quiet run of fountains, and a seasonal Romanesque pool that asks only that you do very little. Mornings arrive as a full breakfast, cooked to order. Afternoons, as something just out of the oven.

IV The Season

You didn't come
for a hotel.

You came for the season. We're a block and a half from it.

  1. Mid-July – Labor Day

    The Meet

    The first race goes off in mid-July and the town doesn't exhale until Labor Day. Saratoga Race Course has run on Union Avenue since 1863 — the oldest organized major sporting venue in America, and the closest thing racing has to a cathedral. The Travers caps it in August, as it has since 1864. From our porch, it's a walk, not a drive.

  2. All Summer

    The Stage

    When the horses rest, the music starts. The Saratoga Performing Arts Center — summer home of New York City Ballet and the Philadelphia Orchestra — is minutes away, and Libby keeps the kitchen on for the after-concert hour.

  3. Year-Round

    The Waters

    Before the horses, there were the springs. Saratoga was the Queen of the Spas, and people came to take the waters long before they came to bet on them. The mineral baths still run, year-round, a few minutes from the porch — which is the whole argument for coming in the quiet months.

  4. Autumn, Winter, Spring

    The Quiet Months

    Autumn turns the historic district gold. Winter empties the streets and lights the fires. Spring brings the gardens back. The season is short. The reasons to come are not.

From the House

From the house.

A page of the innkeepers' notebook, kept irregularly, as such things are.

August · The Meet

The early post.

The early post pulls the house up before the kitchen does — boots on the stair, a thermos filled at the door, the porch already taken by people who mean to walk the block and a half to the gate. Coffee first, the first race after. There is no hurrying either one, and no need to.

January · The Quiet Months

The case for the off-season.

The track is dark and the streets belong to whoever is out in them, which in January is almost no one. The mineral springs run as they have since before the horses; the historic district keeps its snow; the fires are lit by mid-afternoon and stay that way. We have always thought the quiet months were the better-kept secret, and we are in no rush to correct it.

A Saturday · At Libby

One seating, by candlelight.

Six courses, one seating, the room lit low and the menu settled days earlier with whatever the growers sent. The table fills, the candles do the rest, and the evening keeps its own time. Nobody asks for the check until well after they should.

VI Weddings & Gatherings

One house.
One party. Yours.

We only ever host one at a time.

An acre of gardens, a wraparound porch, a seasonal pool, and a Victorian house that has been practicing for this since 1901. Take the whole of it — every room, every chair on the porch, the gardens at dusk — and let Libby cook. We don't run weddings off a banquet menu and a floor plan; we close the gates and hand you the house.

The Table

Dinner is Libby's: a six-course supper for your people, in the room where we serve it every night. No ballroom, no chafing dishes, no other party in the next hall. Just the table, the candles, and the kind of evening the house was built to keep.

The Quiet Part

Small by design. A wedding, an anniversary, a milestone someone would rather not announce. We're good at discretion — it's most of what we do.

Arrival & the porch

Park at the door — there is room for your car where you leave it. Whoever is on the desk will be the one to meet you, and the porch is yours from the moment you set the bags down.

A full breakfast, cooked to order and served until ten. Something out of the oven turns up again in the afternoon, without being announced.

All eight rooms keep a place for one. Let us know before you arrive, and we'll have the room ready for two of you.

04

Getting here

About three hours by car from New York, Boston, or Montreal. From the front steps it is a block and a half to the track gate, and the better part of the town is on foot from there.

The meet runs from mid-July to Labor Day, and those weeks fill first. If your dates fall inside it, the sooner you write the better the room.
