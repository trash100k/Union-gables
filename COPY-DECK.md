# Union Gables — Phase-1 Copy Deck & Design Logic

*Production copy in the house voice (`PRD.md §5`), each block paired with the design logic
that should carry it. Prepared June 2026.*

**How to read this deck**
- **Copy** = ready to set, in voice. **Design logic** = how it should be laid out, paced, and moved.
- `〈confirm〉` flags a room-specific physical detail I've inferred — validate with the property
  before publishing (accuracy is the affluent-trust currency; `PRD.md §5.5`).
- Voice reminder: assume don't justify; understate; never beg; provenance over features.

## A. The Rooms

### Section intro
> **A handful of rooms, each with a name.**
> Oversized chambers and suites in a house built in 1901 — every one with its own bath, its
> own fire, and antiques chosen for the room rather than the catalogue. No two are alike.
> Most people come back for the same one.

**Design logic.** One declarative headline, one paragraph, then the grid. No amenity icons up
top — provenance first, features later. The line *"Most people come back for the same one"*
does the conversion work scarcity banners usually do, without raising its voice. Set the
intro at ~620px max measure so it reads as editorial, not marketing.

---

### The Annie  *(turret · king · fireplace)*
> The turret room, west side, with windows on three sides and the afternoon coming in late.
> A king under luxury linen, marble-topped tables, orientals wall to wall, and a fire for the
> evenings that turn. The light is the reason people ask for it by name.

**Design logic.** Annie is the flagship — lead the grid with it and let its plate run slightly
larger. Real photo of the turret light at golden hour; if the photo lands, the monogram fades
out (already built). Tag line is three words, letter-spaced, gold — a caption, not a spec list.
*〈confirm〉 three-window turret, king, marble tables, orientals — all documented; safe.*

### The Kate  *(suite · garden view · fireplace)*
> A corner suite that looks down on the garden, with room to sit and a fire to sit by. Quiet
> at the back of the house, which is exactly why it goes first.

**Design logic.** Photograph toward the window and the garden beyond — depth signals space, and
space signals money far better than the word "suite." Keep the body to two sentences; the
suite earns the white space. *〈confirm〉 corner, garden-facing, sitting area, fireplace.*

### The Cindy  *(queen · four-poster · porchside)*
> Closest to the porch, and to the smell of baking that drifts up around four o'clock. A
> four-poster, a reading chair set to the sun, and the kind of warmth a house only gets with age.

**Design logic.** This is the *sensory* room in the set — the copy leads with smell and warmth,
not furniture. Pair with a detail shot (the chair, the light) rather than a wide. *〈confirm〉
four-poster, porch-adjacent.*

### The Edward  *(king · fireplace)*
> A broad, level-headed room near the front of the house — good light, a deep bed, a fire, and
> the street's old maples for company.

**Design logic.** "Level-headed" gives a masculine, settled register without saying so. Wide,
calm photograph; symmetrical crop. *〈confirm〉 front-facing, king, the maples.*

### The Bill  *(king · fireplace · soaking bath)*
> A generous room with a bath worth the trip upstairs. Antiques, a fire, and the unhurried
> proportions of a house that was built before anyone was counting square feet.

**Design logic.** Lead the photo with the bath (we have the asset). The line about square feet
is a quiet flex at modern hotels — keep it deadpan. *〈confirm〉 notable bath, king.*

### The Bruce  *(queen · fireplace)*
> Snug, warm, and low-lit — a room for reading late and sleeping in later. The fire does most
> of the talking.

**Design logic.** Short copy, darker photographic treatment, the one genuinely cozy room.
Let the plate run a touch moodier than its neighbours. *〈confirm〉 size, mood.*

### The Linda  *(queen · fireplace · garden side)*
> On the garden side, soft and green in the morning. Period furniture, a fire for the
> shoulder seasons, and birdsong you'll pretend you didn't enjoy.

**Design logic.** The wry last clause is the "earned dryness" lever — one per few rooms, no more.
Greenery-forward photo. *〈confirm〉 garden side.*

### The Tom  *(king · fireplace)*
> Plain-spoken and well-made, the way the best rooms in an old house tend to be. A fire, good
> linen, and nothing you have to figure out.

**Design logic.** "Nothing you have to figure out" answers a real affluent anxiety — friction —
without naming it. Clean, uncluttered photograph. *〈confirm〉 king.*

### The Jane  *(queen · fireplace)*
> Bright and unfussy, with the morning on the good side of the house and a fire for when it
> isn't morning anymore.

**Design logic.** The lightest, most feminine register in the set — high-key photo, airy crop.
*〈confirm〉 light orientation.*

### The Jody  *(queen · fireplace)*
> A quiet room that keeps to itself, for the kind of stay where you'd rather not be found.

**Design logic.** One sentence. The most restrained entry in the grid — and often the most
intriguing for it. Let the white space and a single detail shot do everything. *〈confirm〉.*

### The Michael  *(king · fireplace)*
> Steady, handsome, and easy to settle into — a fire, a deep bed, and the good manners to
> leave you alone.

**Design logic.** "The good manners to leave you alone" = discretion as a feature. Warm,
masculine palette in the photo. *〈confirm〉 king.*

### The Henry Suite  *(suite · private entrance · poolside · carriage house)*
> On the garden level of the carriage house, with its own door onto the pool — a suite for
> people who like the house but love their privacy. Come and go as you please; no one's counting.

**Design logic.** Sell the **private entrance** — for affluent guests, autonomy is the luxury.
Photograph the door and the pool, not the bed. Give it its own slightly-larger card and a
"private entrance" tag in the accent silk color. *〈confirm〉 garden-level carriage house, private
entrance by the pool — documented.*

### The Carriage House  *(two rooms · living area · most private)*
> The whole of the carriage house — a bedroom, a proper living area, and the most distance
> the property can put between you and everyone else. The one to take for a week, or a season.

**Design logic.** This is the **longest-stay / highest-value** unit — copy explicitly invites
"a week, or a season" (the meet). Two-photo layout (living + bedroom). Position last in the
grid as the crescendo. *〈confirm〉 separate living area, two rooms.*

> **Closing line under the grid:**
> Eight rooms keep a place for the dog. Ask, and we'll make the bed for two and a half.

**Design logic.** Keep this exact line — it's the deck's best single proof of voice: a real
amenity (pet-friendly) delivered as wit, never as a bullet.

## B. The Season  *(new page)*

### Hero
> **You didn't come for a hotel.**
> You came for the season. We're a block and a half from it.

### The Meet
> The first race goes off in mid-July and the town doesn't exhale until Labor Day. Saratoga
> Race Course has run on Union Avenue since 1863 — the oldest organized major sporting venue
> in America, and the closest thing racing has to a cathedral. The Travers caps it in August,
> as it has since 1864. From our porch, it's a walk, not a drive.

### The Stage
> When the horses rest, the music starts. The Saratoga Performing Arts Center — summer home of
> New York City Ballet and the Philadelphia Orchestra — is minutes away, and Libby keeps the
> kitchen on for the after-concert hour.

### The Waters
> Before the horses, there were the springs. Saratoga was the Queen of the Spas, and people
> came to take the waters long before they came to bet on them. The mineral baths still run,
> year-round, a few minutes from the porch — which is the whole argument for coming in the
> quiet months.

### The Quiet Months
> Autumn turns the historic district gold. Winter empties the streets and lights the fires.
> Spring brings the gardens back. The season is short. The reasons to come are not.

**Design logic.**
- **Structure as a calendar, not a brochure.** Four movements — Meet, Stage, Waters, Quiet —
  laid out as a vertical scroll-driven timeline so the *rhythm of the year* is the layout.
- **Accuracy on display.** "Oldest organized major sporting venue" is set in the body, not
  hedged in a footnote — the precision is the flex (`PRD.md §5.5`). Never "oldest racetrack."
- **The racing-silk accent earns its one appearance here** — a single ribbon of deep cherry or
  royal on "The Meet," used like a jockey's colors, nowhere else on the page.
- **Off-season is a sales argument, not an apology.** "The Quiet Months" is given equal visual
  weight to "The Meet" — same type scale, same photographic richness — because the business
  problem is seasonality and the page is where we solve it.
- **Photography:** the track gate from the street (proximity made literal), a candlelit Libby
  table, the Roosevelt-bath/springs mood, foliage over the porch. Golden hour throughout.

## C. Weddings & Gatherings  *(new page)*

### Hero
> **One house. One party. Yours.**
> We only ever host one at a time.

### Body
> An acre of gardens, a wraparound porch, a seasonal pool, and a Victorian house that has been
> practicing for this since 1901. Take the whole of it — every room, every chair on the porch,
> the gardens at dusk — and let Libby cook. We don't run weddings off a banquet menu and a
> floor plan; we close the gates and hand you the house.

### The Table
> Dinner is Libby's: a six-course supper for your people, in the room where we serve it every
> night. No ballroom, no chafing dishes, no other party in the next hall. Just the table, the
> candles, and the kind of evening the house was built to keep.

### The Quiet Part
> Small by design. A wedding, an anniversary, a milestone someone would rather not announce.
> We're good at discretion — it's most of what we do.

> **Inquiry line (button + form intro):**
> Tell us the dates and the headcount. We'll tell you if the house is free.

**Design logic.**
- **Lead with exclusivity-by-fact, not by adjective.** "We only ever host one at a time" is the
  entire pitch — buyout intimacy — and it never uses the word *exclusive* (banned; reads new-money).
- **"The Quiet Part" sells discretion as the product.** This is the highest-margin, most
  affluence-aligned section; give it real estate and a hushed, low-light photographic treatment.
- **Inquiry, not booking.** The CTA is a conversation ("we'll tell you if the house is free"),
  matching how this clientele actually transacts — by relationship, not by cart.
- **Photography:** the gardens at dusk, the porch set for dinner, the candlelit table — never a
  staged "wedding stock" shot. People implied (a hand, a glass), faces rare.

## D. Reserve  *(the close)*

### Section
> **The season is short. The porch is not.**
> We keep the house small on purpose and we don't oversell. If the dates are open, they're yours.

### Direct-booking nudge (stated once, dryly)
> Book with us directly — it's the same room, a better rate, and a person who knows your name
> when you call. *(518) 584·1558.*

### The persistent "Reserve" affordance (site-wide)
> **Reserve** → opens the booking step. No second verb, no "now," no badge.

**Design logic.**
- The close is a **permission, not a push** — the reader decides they belong here. Centered,
  generous vertical space, one gold button. The address and phone sit as quiet serif lines, not
  a contact-card module.
- **Direct-booking** is the one place we make an argument, and we make it *once* — rate + the
  human ("a person who knows your name"). Affluent guests pay for being known; lead with that,
  not the discount.
- The site-wide **Reserve** control is understated and always present (top-right + a subtle
  return at section ends) so the path is frictionless even though the tone is unhurried. Real
  availability behind it — the spell breaks if "Reserve" leads to a dead form.

## E. Transactional copy  *(the voice doesn't stop at the button)*

**Room confirmation — subject:** *We've kept it for you.*
> Your room is held: **[Room] · [dates]**. The porch, the fire, and breakfast cooked to order
> are all accounted for. Come as you are; we'll do the rest. — Union Gables, 55 Union Avenue.

**Pre-arrival (a few days out) — subject:** *The house is ready when you are.*
> A note before you come. Park at the door; we'll find you. Breakfast runs [hours], the fires
> are laid, and if you're here for the meet, the gate is a block and a half left out the porch.
> Tell us your arrival hour and anything we should know — a fire lit early, a quiet table at
> Libby, a bowl for the dog.

**Post-stay — subject:** *The porch will be here.*
> Thank you for staying. The house is quieter without you in it. When the season turns again —
> or when you'd rather it didn't — the porch will be here, and so will we.

**Libby reservation confirmation — subject:** *The table is set.*
> **[Party size] · [date] · one seating.** Six courses, ours to choose, yours to linger over.
> Come hungry and unhurried. Dietary notes? Reply and we'll see to it.

**Meet-season waitlist — subject:** *The house is full — but seasons turn.*
> We're fully kept for those dates; the meet does that. Say the word and we'll hold your place
> for a cancellation, or find you a quieter week with the same fire and a better rate.

**Design logic.** Plain-text-feeling, serif-forward HTML emails — no loud banners, no stock
hero, no "MANAGE YOUR RESERVATION" button farm. One quiet gold rule, the gable mark, the
address. The waitlist email is the proof-of-voice: a sold-out message that *keeps* the guest
(off-season redirect) instead of dead-ending them, and never apologizes twice.

## F. System & microcopy  *(affluence shows up in the small places)*

| Surface | Copy | Design logic |
|---|---|---|
| Nav CTA | **Reserve** | One word. Authority, not urgency. |
| Secondary hero CTA | **Wander In** | Invites exploration in the house's own idiom. |
| Libby CTA | **A Table at Libby** | "A table," not "Book now." |
| Newsletter | *Notes from the house, now and then. No noise.* | Permission-respecting; promises restraint. |
| 404 | **That room doesn't exist. The others do.** → *Back to the house.* | Wit + a way home; stays in character. |
| Form placeholders | *Your name · When you'd come · Anything we should know* | Conversational, not "First Name*". |
| Empty/availability | *Those dates are spoken for. Try the week on either side.* | Dignified, offers a path, never "SOLD OUT". |
| Cookie/consent | *We keep only what helps us keep the house. No selling you on.* | Discretion as brand, even in legalese. |

## Cross-cutting design logic (the rules under all of the above)

1. **One idea per screen.** Affluence reads as *space*. Generous whitespace and a single
   thought per viewport signal confidence; density signals a sales floor.
2. **Provenance first, features second.** Heritage and specifics lead; amenity detail lives one
   layer down (a drawer, a hover) for those who look. Never an icon row above the story.
3. **Motion drifts and settles** — never bounces or pops. The Three.js dust, the scroll reveals,
   parallax: all "dust in sun / the field after a race." Reduced-motion fully honored.
4. **The racing silk is a ribbon, not a theme.** One disciplined accent color, used only in
   racing/Libby contexts (The Meet, a single tag), so it *means* something when it appears.
5. **Photography is golden-hour, candlelit, and brass-detailed** — porch, gardens, turrets, the
   Libby table. People rare and implied. Never hotel-stock.
6. **Two seasonal states.** A warmer/busier meet-season skin and a quieter/fireside off-season
   skin — even a single swapped hero line earns its keep and fights the seasonality problem.
7. **The path is frictionless even when the tone is slow.** Unhurried voice, persistent and
   real booking affordance. Calm is the feeling; dead-ends are the only sin.
